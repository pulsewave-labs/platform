## Module 1: Supply & Demand

### S1 — Understanding Price Action
- Price action = market participant psychology illustrated through candlesticks
- Japanese candlesticks: open, close, high, low
- Body size = conviction. Large body = confidence, small body = indecision
- Long lower wick = seller exhaustion, buyers stepping in
- Long upper wick = buyer exhaustion, sellers stepping in
- Key single candles: spinning tops (indecision), hammer/hanging man (reversal), marubozu (dominant), doji family (indecision/exhaustion)
- Gravestone doji = sellers in control, dragonfly doji = buyers in control
- Reading candles = reading the storyboard of buyer/seller psychology

### S2 — Components of a Market
- Market = where buyers and sellers exchange (virtual exchanges now)
- Buyers = Demand, Sellers = Supply
- Goal: identify exhaustion points of either party
- Supply zones = where sellers step in (resistance areas with wicks showing rejection)
- Demand zones = where buyers step in (support areas with long lower wicks)
- **Finite supply/demand**: demand zones have limited buyers → eventual breakdown; supply zones have limited sellers → eventual breakout
- Ping-pong effect: price bounces between supply and demand zones
- As a trader: buy at demand zones, sell at supply zones

### S3 — Support & Resistance
- S/R = physical illustration of supply and demand through price action
- **"Be a buyer at support and a seller at resistance"** — core principle
- Two types: diagonal (trendlines) and horizontal (zones)
- Valid trendline = 3+ touches along the line
- Trendlines subjective (body-to-body vs wick-to-wick) — consistency matters more than method
- Horizontal S/R = zones, not exact lines
- **Rule of Fives**: S/R weakens with each retest. After ~5 touches, likely to break
- **S/R Flips**: resistance becomes support (and vice versa) after a break — key access points for traders

## Module 2: Identifying Trends

### S1 — Trending Markets
- Uptrend = higher highs + higher lows (buyers in control)
- Downtrend = lower highs + lower lows (sellers in control)
- Exhaustion = pivot point where buyer/seller loses control → potential reversal
- **"The trend is your friend"** — trading against the trend is like swimming upstream
- Trends within trends: daily uptrend may contain hourly downtrends
- Technical analysis is always contextual to timeframe
- Higher lows often occur at previous highs = S/R flip confirmation
- Trends are finite — never assume continuation indefinitely

### S2 — Rangebound Markets
- Range = price bouncing between a high point (resistance) and low point (support)
- Characteristics: horizontal S/R, multiple touches confirm the range
- Range high = sellers/supply, Range low = buyers/demand
- **More touches = closer to exhaustion** of either side
- Range = consolidation phase, typically precedes breakout or breakdown
- **Midpoint** of range is significant — lots of price interaction there
- **Fakeouts/deviations**: price can temporarily break range boundaries before snapping back
- Range low/high are NOT lines in the sand — they're zones
- Within ranges, look for micro-trends (uptrend within range = breakout likely)
- Rule of fives applies: 5th touch of range boundary often leads to break

## Module 4: Timeframe Analysis

### S2 — HTF Scenario (Litecoin example)
- **Start with the highest timeframe** (monthly) → mark key S/R → drop to weekly → daily
- Monthly levels hold the most weight
- Mark swing points and S/R zones on monthly first
- Drop to weekly to add more detail
- Drop to daily for candle structure analysis and entry timing
- Use TradingView replay tool to practice (can't see future)
- **S/R flips are key entry points** — when resistance becomes support, look for longs
- Candlestick structure at key levels tells the story (long lower wicks at support = buyers present)
- Consolidation zones at key levels lead to continuation or reversal
- Track market structure (HH/HL or LH/LL) across timeframes
- Position traders use monthly/weekly levels; scalpers/day traders use the same concept on lower TFs

## Module 5: Risk Management

### S1 — Risk Management
- **"Risk management is the single most important skill"**
- Technical analysis helps you make money; risk management helps you KEEP it
- Position sizing formula: `Position Size = (Equity × Risk%) / StopLoss%`
- 1% risk per trade = 100 trades before bankruptcy = 99 more chances
- All-in = one bad trade ends your career
- General rule: 1-5% risk per trade
- **Risk = maximum you're willing to lose; Return = expected gain**
- Think in percentages, not dollars — this nurtures a professional mindset
- Risk:Reward ratio (R:R) = expected return / risk
- R multiple = actual outcome (may differ from expected R:R)
- "Traders are not fortune tellers — we never know the outcome of any single trade"
- **"Preservation of capital comes before profitability"**

### S2 — Achieving Profitability
- Profitability = function of win rate × average R multiple
- Three profitable categories: high WR + low R, low WR + high R, high WR + high R
- Break-even formula: at R=1, need 50% WR; at R=2, need only 33% WR; at R=0.5, need 67% WR
- **Asymmetric setups**: strive for R multiples > 1 on every trade
- Drawdown recovery is non-linear: 5% loss needs 5.3% gain; 20% loss needs 25% gain; 50% loss needs 100% gain
- **Losing streaks are inevitable** — even at 50% WR, 3% chance of 5 consecutive losses
- Compounding example: $10K, 55% WR, 2R avg, 2% risk = profitable system
- **"The Holy Grail of trading does not exist"** — but frameworks that utilize odds in your favor do

### S3 — Optimizing Returns
- Kelly Criterion: scientific approach to optimal position sizing
- Basic: K = 2P - 1 (where P = win probability)
- Modified for varying R multiples: K = (B×P - Q) / B (where B = R multiple, Q = 1-P)
- Raw Kelly often gives aggressive numbers (e.g., 40% risk) — too dangerous
- **Pareto's Principle (80/20)**: 80% of trades are throwaways; 20% are home runs (or big losers)
- Multiply Kelly output by 20% → much safer position sizing (e.g., 40% → 8%)
- **Journaling is essential**: document trade parameters, R:R, actual R multiple, entry/stop/target, screenshots, emotions, sleep, hunger
- Without journaling, can't identify strengths/weaknesses or build a system
- "Without journaling we will never be able to implement a framework for risk management"
